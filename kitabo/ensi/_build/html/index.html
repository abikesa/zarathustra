Preface

Preface#

The story of humanity is a story of arcs—of falls and returns. We lose paradise, plunge into hell, and seek our way back. This book explores that journey through the ancient Jewish concepts of tikkun atzmi (self-repair) and tikkun olam (repairing the world), navigating the realms of Olam HaZeh (this world) and Olam HaBa (the world to come). These concepts are mirrored in the allegories of Paradise, Earth, and Hell—not just as places, but as stages in the cycles of human experience. In each stage, our alliances—whether with God, neighbor, or self—shape our journey back to grace.

Robert Aumann’s “Nash Program” sheds light on the strategic dynamics that define this journey. By mapping out cooperative, iterative, and adversarial games, he offers a lens to understand how humans navigate conflicts and form alliances. At the core of every arc is the question of salvation: does one find it through divine intervention, through solidarity with others, or through self-reliance? This echoes through the narratives crafted by filmmakers like Woody Allen, the Coen Brothers, Quentin Tarantino, Wes Anderson, and Tyler Perry—directors who delve into the fragile pacts humans make, shaped by the echoes of their personal histories.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def draw_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle given vertices and labels for each vertex with matching color."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=color)  # Set label color

def get_triangle_vertices_3d(center, radius, perspective_scale, tilt):
    """
    Returns the vertices of a tilted equilateral triangle for a 3D effect.
    `perspective_scale` shrinks the triangle to simulate depth.
    `tilt` applies a slight rotation for perspective effect.
    """
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi/2  # angles for vertices of an equilateral triangle
    vertices = np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * perspective_scale * np.cos(angles + tilt),
                                center[1] + radius * perspective_scale * np.sin(angles + tilt)])
    return vertices

# Create the plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.set_aspect('equal')

# Define the layers of the fractal with vertices and labels
centers = [(0, 0)]
radii = [2.5, 6, 10]
triads = [
    ['Betrayal', 'Power       ', '      Survival'],
    ['Loyalty', 'Empathy', 'Resilience'],
    ['Faith', 'Love', 'Hope']
]

# Set the color scheme: blood red, green, sky blue
colors = ['lightsalmon', 'lightgreen', 'paleturquoise']

# 3D perspective parameters: smaller scale as the fractal moves inward (simulating depth)
scales = [.5, .75, 1]  # simulate depth
tilts = [0, np.pi / 12, np.pi / 6]  # slight rotation for perspective

# Draw the triangles with increasing radius and perspective scaling
for radius, triad, color, scale, tilt in zip(radii, triads, colors, scales, tilts):
    vertices = get_triangle_vertices_3d(centers[0], radius, scale, tilt)
    draw_triangle(ax, vertices, triad, color=color)

Yet, as Aumann reminds us, we cannot afford to be wide-eyed idealists. Even Isaiah’s vision—of a time when swords would be beaten into ploughshares—acknowledges the conditions necessary for true peace. “They shall beat their swords into ploughshares,” Isaiah says, but only “when there is a central government—a Lord, recognized by all.” Without this, peace remains a fragile truce, with swords always lurking in the background, ready to be unsheathed. This is the paradox of human nature: the tension between our yearning for cooperation and the reality of our preparedness for conflict.

Ivan Karamazov similarly lamented about humanity’s need for “a Lord”:

For these pitiful creatures are concerned not only to find what one or the other can worship, but to find something that all would believe in and worship; what is essential is that all may be together in it. This craving for community of worship is the chief misery of every man individually and of all humanity from the beginning of time. For the sake of common worship they’ve slain each other with the sword.

As Zarathustra lamented the complacency of the last men, so too does this book challenge the easy comforts of superficial harmony. Real transformation—whether personal or societal—demands grappling with chaos and facing the hard truths of adversarial dynamics. It requires the wisdom to know when to reach for peace and when to keep the sword in hand.

In each chapter, we will explore the arc of fall and return, examining how salvation is not a single path but a complex interplay of relationships, strategies, and conditions. It is time to plant the germ of our highest hope—but with eyes open to the reality that peace, like the arc of human life, must be earned and guarded. For amidst our longing for the mountain of the Lord, we must remember: paradise is not regained without a reckoning with the swords that remain unbroken.
