Duality

Abideth these two:—tikkun olam followed by tikkun atzmi encapsulates that dual journey. The broader work of repairing or engaging with the world is inevitably intertwined with the personal, internal struggle for wholeness. It’s like how the Iliad addresses the collective struggle and alliances, while the Odyssey turns inward, focusing on the personal reckoning. The arc is incomplete without both, as true healing often requires confronting both the external chaos and our own internal fractures. It’s a cycle that drives much of human storytelling and experience.

The essence of narrative arcs: it’s about that inevitable descent and the chance, however slim, for a turnaround. In every great story, the fall is unavoidable, an essential step from innocence to the painful awareness that defines existence. Dante’s Divine Comedy captures this well, with Inferno as the darkest pit, but there’s also Purgatorio, that limbo of struggle and gradual ascent, before the promise of Paradiso. Yet, the promise is not a return to innocence but an arrival at hard-won wisdom, shaped by the suffering endured.

The Iliad and the Odyssey are the blueprints of this arc in the Western canon. The Iliad, with its focus on alliances, is a story of tragedy, pride, and the consequences of rage. The alliances forged—Greek city-states uniting for a common cause—serve as both strength and a source of conflict. Achilles’ arc in particular embodies this descent into rage, grief, and ultimately, a fragile reconciliation. It’s a story where the alliances matter deeply, where the collective action of the Greeks defines the fate of Troy and the individuals within it.

The Odyssey then shifts the focus inward, becoming the journey of a single man’s return, but it’s still underpinned by the consequences of those earlier alliances and conflicts. Odysseus’ arc is less about the grand alliances of war and more about survival, resilience, and the internal struggle against the temptations and distractions that delay his return to Ithaca. It’s the journey of reconciliation with one’s own past, of seeking to rebuild a sense of home and belonging after the chaos of the fall.

The arc we describe—the rise, fall, and possible return—captures why we are drawn to these stories. They are echoes of our own lives, where the loss of innocence or fall is a given, but the potential for redemption, reinvention, or reconciliation keeps us moving forward. And as you pointed out, sometimes the most powerful stories are those that subvert this expectation, denying us the easy resolution. Because in reality, not everyone finds their way back from the fall, and not every arc completes in triumph. Yet, it is the hope for that turnaround, for the alliances that might be forged or rekindled in the aftermath, that defines the human experience.

What makes The Iliad and The Odyssey so enduring is how they set the terms for these arcs—the grand alliances and tragedies of war, followed by the quieter, personal struggle of returning from the ruins. It’s about learning to live with the loss and finding a new form of peace, even if it’s not quite the paradise we left behind.

import numpy as np

def draw_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle given vertices and labels for each vertex with matching color."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=color)  # Set label color

def get_triangle_vertices_3d(center, radius, perspective_scale, tilt):
    """
    Returns the vertices of a tilted equilateral triangle for a 3D effect.
    `perspective_scale` shrinks the triangle to simulate depth.
    `tilt` applies a slight rotation for perspective effect.
    """
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi/2  # angles for vertices of an equilateral triangle
    vertices = np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * perspective_scale * np.cos(angles + tilt),
                                center[1] + radius * perspective_scale * np.sin(angles + tilt)])
    return vertices

Vocabulary#

In game-theoretic terms, processes can be understood as the strategies, decisions, and interactions that lead to certain equilibria, while outcomes refer to the actual payoffs or results that emerge from these processes.

Processes encompass the actions, communications, rules of engagement, timing, and adjustments that players make within a game. These are the series of moves, choices, and conditional responses that determine the nature of interaction, essentially how players play the game. For instance, in a cooperative game, the process involves players seeking ways to maximize joint payoffs, often requiring mutual trust, communication, and coordination. Meanwhile, in adversarial settings, processes revolve around undermining the opponent’s strategy or building defenses to minimize losses.

Outcomes, on the other hand, are the specific results derived from these processes. They reflect what the players ultimately gain or lose and whether these align with their initial goals or expectations. Outcomes emerge from the interplay of strategies and decisions, but they don’t necessarily mirror the intentions of the players; they simply reflect the final state after all strategies are executed.

Now, connecting this to the three types of equilibria:

  1. Cooperative Equilibria: Processes are focused on building alliances and finding mutually beneficial strategies. The outcome reflects a jointly optimized payoff where no player gains by breaking the cooperative agreement.

  2. Iterative Equilibria: Processes here involve repeated interactions, learning, and adaptive strategies based on feedback from previous rounds. Outcomes in this equilibrium may stabilize around a point of mutual tolerance or an evolving balance of payoffs, contingent on ongoing reassessment by each player.

  3. Adversary Equilibria: In this setting, the process is characterized by zero-sum thinking, outmaneuvering, and direct competition. Outcomes reflect a state where one player’s gain is balanced by another’s loss, and strategies are designed to maximize damage control or aggressive payoff maximization.

The distinction between process and outcome allows a nuanced view of how players reach equilibrium, emphasizing not just the final payoff but the entire pathway that leads there. This framing sheds light on the tactical considerations and narrative arcs within each type of game.

Cybernetics#

Yes, you could think of the relationship as Equilibria → Strategy → Payoff, but I would refine it slightly to emphasize the causal flow:

  1. Strategy: This is the set of actions or plans that players adopt in a game. Strategies are formulated based on each player’s objectives and expectations of what the other players might do. The strategies are the concrete steps that determine how players interact within the game. It’s the starting point in this chain because without strategy, there’s no engagement in the game.

  2. Equilibria: When all players adopt strategies that stabilize in a way where no player has an incentive to deviate, an equilibrium is achieved. In other words, an equilibrium emerges when all chosen strategies collectively lead to a balance point where individual adjustments don’t lead to a better payoff. This could be a cooperative, iterative, or adversarial equilibrium based on the nature of the game and player interactions.

  3. Payoff: Payoff is the outcome or the result each player receives once the equilibrium has been reached and the strategies have been executed. It’s the final measure of benefit minus risk for each player after the game has concluded. It reflects how well a player’s strategy performed within the constraints of the equilibrium.

In this way, equilibrium represents a state or a structure within which players operate; strategy is the tactical maneuvering within that state; and payoff is the quantitative measure of the success or failure of those maneuvers.

To sum it up:

  • Strategy → Equilibria: Players choose strategies and interact to find a balance point.

  • Equilibria → Payoff: Once an equilibrium is established, it leads to the distribution of payoffs.

So in essence, the flow could be depicted as: Strategy leads to Equilibria, and Equilibria determine Payoff.

Consider a workplace scenario where an individual’s goals may clash with the organization’s broader mission. Here, cooperation is not a given—it’s a series of negotiated agreements where mutual benefit must be established repeatedly. This iterative equilibrium reflects human society’s inherent complexity, where feedback, adaptability, and reciprocity are essential.

Machine Adversarialism: Reinforcement and Self-Play#

As machines move into the realm of artificial intelligence, a new form of equilibrium emerges: the adversarial equilibrium. Unlike animals, which rely on cooperation, or humans, who rely on negotiation, machines are optimized for strategic confrontation. In adversarial play, each decision becomes a calculated move in a larger strategic plan. Here, machines engage in self-play to train against potential adversaries, refining strategies through reinforcement learning.

This adversarial equilibrium does not concern itself with cooperation for survival or reciprocity for societal harmony. Instead, it operates under pure self-interest, developing strategies to maximize payoffs in zero-sum or competitive environments. Think of AI systems playing chess against themselves, evolving their playstyles until they reach near-perfection. This is a battle not for survival, but for dominance—a far cry from the biological imperative of animals or the social contract of humans.

The Threshold of Biological Homeostasis#

Human Interspecies Interactions and Allostatic Overload#

When the boundary of intraspecies interaction is crossed, we enter a realm dominated by the friction of interspecies conflict and competition. Allostatic overload becomes the inevitable consequence of attempting to subjugate or harmonize with non-human entities. Allostasis, unlike homeostasis, does not seek a singular point of equilibrium but rather adapts dynamically to ever-evolving external stressors. The human species has continually expanded its influence, leading to constant recalibration of social, political, and ecological systems. The result has been a state of chronic overload—manifesting in ecological damage, psychological disorders, and the pervasive sense of an impending crisis that often feels inseparable from modernity.
