Game Theory (Hidden)#

So let‚Äôs organize the ideas within our framework of strategy, payoff, and equilibrium & with language borrowed from machine-learning, which at best is grounded in self-play, adversarial games (Inferno), and unsupervised reinforcement learning:

  1. Strategy/Allostasis (Model Parameters: Weights & Biases)

    • In game theory, the ‚Äústrategy‚Äù is what players choose to maximize their payoff.

    • In neural networks, the strategy is the parameters of the model: weights and biases. These are what the network adjusts to minimize the loss during training. The model starts with random values and ‚Äúlearns‚Äù better values through training.

    • Visualization: Imagine this as a network of nodes with connections (weights) between them. As we adjust these weights, the flow of information through the network changes, just like tweaking a player‚Äôs strategy.

  2. Payoff/Exostasis (Loss Function)

    • In game theory, the payoff measures how well a strategy worked for a player.

    • In neural networks, the ‚Äúpayoff‚Äù is your loss function, often measuring how far off the predictions are from the true labels (e.g., mean squared error for regression or cross-entropy for classification). Backpropagation adjusts the model to reduce this loss.

    • Visualization: Think of this like a terrain of mountains and valleys (the ‚Äúloss landscape‚Äù). The higher the terrain, the worse the model‚Äôs predictions, and the lower the terrain, the better. The model tries to move ‚Äúdownhill‚Äù in this landscape to minimize the loss.

  3. Equilibrium/Homeostasis (Convergence, Local or Global Minimum)

    • In game theory, equilibrium is the point where no player can improve their payoff by changing their strategy.

    • In neural networks, equilibrium corresponds to convergence‚Äîwhen the model reaches a point where further training doesn‚Äôt significantly reduce the loss. This is usually a local minimum (or maximum in some cases) where the model stabilizes.

    • Visualization: Equilibrium is when the model has found a low point in the loss landscape. This could be a global minimum (the lowest point) or a local minimum (a lower point that‚Äôs not the absolute lowest). If adversarial training is used, this could oscillate, but generally, it is where the model has reached an inflection point.
