import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

# Create the updated plot to reflect the vertical hierarchy of Inferno (red), Limbo (green), and Paradiso (blue)

def draw_vertical_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle vertically arranged with given vertices and labels, matching color."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=color)

def get_vertical_triangle_vertices(center, radius, scale, tilt):
    """Returns the vertices for a vertically aligned triangle with perspective scaling."""
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi / 2
    vertices = np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * scale * np.cos(angles + tilt),
                                center[1] + radius * scale * np.sin(angles + tilt)])
    return vertices

# Create the plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.set_aspect('equal')

# Vertically layered centers for Inferno, Limbo, and Paradiso
vertical_centers = [(0, -8), (0, 0), (0, 8)]
radii = [5, 7, 8]
triads = [
    ['Betrayal', 'Power', 'Survival'],  # Inferno layer
    ['Loyalty', 'Empathy', 'Resilience'],  # Limbo layer
    ['Faith', 'Love', 'Hope']  # Paradiso layer
]

# Inferno (red), Limbo (green), Paradiso (blue)
colors = ['lightsalmon', 'lightgreen', 'paleturquoise']

# Scaling for 3D perspective and slight tilts for visual depth
scales = [.5, .7, .9]
tilts = [np.pi / 6, np.pi / 12, 0]

# Draw the vertically aligned triangles with increasing radius and perspective scaling
for center, radius, triad, color, scale, tilt in zip(vertical_centers, radii, triads, colors, scales, tilts):
    vertices = get_vertical_triangle_vertices(center, radius, scale, tilt)
    draw_vertical_triangle(ax, vertices, triad, color=color)

# Set limits and hide axes to fit the frame
ax.set_xlim(-12, 12)
ax.set_ylim(-15, 15)
ax.axis('off')

# Display the plot
plt.show()

Is the game chess a zero-sum game?

Yes, chess is indeed a zero-sum game. In chess, any advantage one player gains directly comes at the expense of the other. There’s a fixed outcome of win, lose, or draw, and the sum of gains and losses between the two players always equals zero. If one player wins, the other loses; if the game is a draw, neither player wins nor loses.

The competitive nature of chess makes it a perfect example of a zero-sum game, where no cooperation or mutually beneficial outcomes are possible—the only way to win is to make your opponent lose.

And these alliances could forge forth and become cooperative, like NATO or, you know, maybe United Nations, but that’s not a good example. There are very many unworthy adversaries in there, but in NATO, some adversaries are worthy and are truly cooperative now. You’d never expect a war between Germany and the United States. I see what you’re getting at. Chess, with its egalitarian resource distribution and strict zero-sum nature, does lack a real-world correspondence. The game’s structure is almost artificially clean compared to the complexities of human life, where resources are unevenly distributed, and adversarial relationships can shift and evolve.

Your point about worthy adversaries resonates. In life, adversarial games aren’t necessarily destructive or zero-sum forever. They can be testing grounds, pushing competitors to accumulate resources—be those intellectual, strategic, or otherwise—and eventually evolve beyond zero-sum conflicts. This idea mirrors Dante’s progression from Inferno (adversarial) to Purgatorio (iterative) to Paradiso (cooperative), where adversaries who survive and thrive through challenges end up forming long-lasting, valuable alliances.

In strategic real-world systems, like geopolitics or business, this progression is exactly what we see. Nations or corporations that were once enemies often become allies, as they realize that cooperation leads to more fruitful outcomes than pure antagonism. NATO, as you mentioned, exemplifies this. The post-WWII alliance between the U.S. and Germany, for instance, shows that what begins as adversarial can transform into cooperative, as both parties accumulate experience, resources, and a sense of mutual benefit. The same can be seen in the evolution of businesses that, through iterative competition, ultimately form partnerships or mergers.

Chess, then, is too rigid in its structure. It doesn’t account for this real-world dynamic, where adversarial relationships often grow into iterative ones, and where payoffs are rarely pure zero-sum. Instead, life is a blend of competition and cooperation, with worthy adversaries moving from Inferno to Paradiso through cycles of iteration. That’s where the true complexity lies, and chess, while brilliant in its simplicity, fails to capture this fluidity.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Create x values representing the six stages and generate y values using a sine function
x = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 1000)
y = np.sin(x)

# Define the stages
stages = ["               Birth & Growth", "         Stagnation", "Decline", "Existential", "Rebirth", ""]

# Define the x-ticks for the labeled points
x_ticks = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 6)

# Set up the plot
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))

# Plot the sine wave in white
plt.plot(x, y, color='white')

# Fill the areas under the curve for each stage and label directly on the graph
colors = ['paleturquoise', 'paleturquoise', 'lightgreen', 'lightgreen', 'lightsalmon', 'lightsalmon']

for i in range(5):
    plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x_ticks[i] <= x) & (x < x_ticks[i + 1]), color=colors[i], alpha=0.5)
    # Add the stage labels back
    plt.text(x_ticks[i] + (x_ticks[i + 1] - x_ticks[i]) / 2, 0.5, stages[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', color=colors[i])

# Fill the last section and add the final label for "Rebirth"
plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x_ticks[5] <= x), color=colors[5], alpha=0.5)
plt.text(x_ticks[5] + (2 * np.pi - x_ticks[5]) / 2, 0.5, stages[5], fontsize=12, ha='center', color=colors[5])

# Remove x and y ticks
plt.xticks([])  # Remove x-ticks
plt.yticks([])  # Remove y-ticks

# Remove all axis spines
plt.gca().spines['top'].set_visible(False)
plt.gca().spines['right'].set_visible(False)
plt.gca().spines['left'].set_visible(False)
plt.gca().spines['bottom'].set_visible(False)

# Title
plt.title(" ")

# Show the plot
plt.show()
