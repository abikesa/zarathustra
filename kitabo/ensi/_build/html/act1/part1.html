A large and growing literature links high levels of ethnic diversity to low levels of public goods provision. Yet although the empirical connection between ethnic heterogeneity and the underprovision of public goods is widely accepted, there is little consensus on the specific mechanisms through which this relationship operates. We identify three families of mechanisms that link diversity to public goods provision—what we term “preferences,” “technology,” and “strategy selection” mechanisms—and run a series of experimental games that permit us to compare the explanatory power of distinct mechanisms within each of these three families. Results from games conducted with a random sample of 300 subjects from a slum neighborhood of Kampala, Uganda, suggest that successful public goods provision in homogenous ethnic communities can be attributed to a strategy selection mechanism: in similar settings, co-ethnics play cooperative equilibria, whereas non-co-ethnics do not. Vocabulary#

In game-theoretic terms, processes can be understood as the strategies, decisions, and interactions that lead to certain equilibria, while outcomes refer to the actual payoffs or results that emerge from these processes.

Processes encompass the actions, communications, rules of engagement, timing, and adjustments that players make within a game. These are the series of moves, choices, and conditional responses that determine the nature of interaction, essentially how players play the game. For instance, in a cooperative game, the process involves players seeking ways to maximize joint payoffs, often requiring mutual trust, communication, and coordination. Meanwhile, in adversarial settings, processes revolve around undermining the opponent’s strategy or building defenses to minimize losses.

Outcomes, on the other hand, are the specific results derived from these processes. They reflect what the players ultimately gain or lose and whether these align with their initial goals or expectations. Outcomes emerge from the interplay of strategies and decisions, but they don’t necessarily mirror the intentions of the players; they simply reflect the final state after all strategies are executed.

Now, connecting this to the three types of equilibria:

  1. Cooperative Equilibria: Processes are focused on building alliances and finding mutually beneficial strategies. The outcome reflects a jointly optimized payoff where no player gains by breaking the cooperative agreement.

  2. Iterative Equilibria: Processes here involve repeated interactions, learning, and adaptive strategies based on feedback from previous rounds. Outcomes in this equilibrium may stabilize around a point of mutual tolerance or an evolving balance of payoffs, contingent on ongoing reassessment by each player.

  3. Adversary Equilibria: In this setting, the process is characterized by zero-sum thinking, outmaneuvering, and direct competition. Outcomes reflect a state where one player’s gain is balanced by another’s loss, and strategies are designed to maximize damage control or aggressive payoff maximization.

The distinction between process and outcome allows a nuanced view of how players reach equilibrium, emphasizing not just the final payoff but the entire pathway that leads there. This framing sheds light on the tactical considerations and narrative arcs within each type of game.

Cybernetics#

Yes, you could think of the relationship as Equilibria → Strategy → Payoff, but I would refine it slightly to emphasize the causal flow:

  1. Strategy: This is the set of actions or plans that players adopt in a game. Strategies are formulated based on each player’s objectives and expectations of what the other players might do. The strategies are the concrete steps that determine how players interact within the game. It’s the starting point in this chain because without strategy, there’s no engagement in the game.

  2. Equilibria: When all players adopt strategies that stabilize in a way where no player has an incentive to deviate, an equilibrium is achieved. In other words, an equilibrium emerges when all chosen strategies collectively lead to a balance point where individual adjustments don’t lead to a better payoff. This could be a cooperative, iterative, or adversarial equilibrium based on the nature of the game and player interactions.

  3. Payoff: Payoff is the outcome or the result each player receives once the equilibrium has been reached and the strategies have been executed. It’s the final measure of benefit minus risk for each player after the game has concluded. It reflects how well a player’s strategy performed within the constraints of the equilibrium.

In this way, equilibrium represents a state or a structure within which players operate; strategy is the tactical maneuvering within that state; and payoff is the quantitative measure of the success or failure of those maneuvers.

To sum it up:

  • Strategy → Equilibria: Players choose strategies and interact to find a balance point.

  • Equilibria → Payoff: Once an equilibrium is established, it leads to the distribution of payoffs.

So in essence, the flow could be depicted as: Strategy leads to Equilibria, and Equilibria determine Payoff.

Dionysian#

Didn’t nietzsche call Hamlet the Dionysian man? He truly understood things & knowledge kills action!

Nietzsche did indeed refer to Hamlet as the “Dionysian man.” In The Birth of Tragedy, Nietzsche contrasts Hamlet with the typical tragic hero. While a tragic hero acts out of passion, Hamlet is paralyzed by his knowledge and understanding. Nietzsche’s take is that Hamlet, like the Dionysian man, has seen through the veil of existence—he understands the underlying chaos and futility of life, and this insight cripples his ability to act.

In Nietzsche’s view, once someone has grasped the deeper, irrational truths of existence—especially the awareness of the inevitable suffering and meaninglessness that permeates life—action becomes almost impossible. Hamlet’s famous indecision, his constant questioning of life, death, and moral responsibility, reflects this paralysis. For Nietzsche, it’s not cowardice or weakness that stops Hamlet, but a deep, almost existential insight.

Knowledge, in this context, kills action because it strips away the illusions that make action possible. Most people act because they believe their actions have purpose or that they can influence the world meaningfully. The Dionysian man, like Hamlet, sees through these illusions and is left with a sense of the absurd, leading to an inability to act because there’s no false belief or motivation to cling to anymore. Hamlet’s inaction, then, isn’t weakness—it’s an acknowledgment of the tragic reality of existence, a theme Nietzsche returns to often in his work.

This parallels Nietzsche’s idea of amor fati, or the love of one’s fate. Only by embracing life’s inherent meaninglessness and chaos can one rise above paralysis and return to action—but not in the naive, purposive way most people act. Rather, it’s action in defiance of absurdity, an acknowledgment that meaning is created, not discovered.

Pendulum#

Where does most humanity reside in terms of cooperation, adversarial, or limbo?

Most of humanity resides in a kind of limbo—somewhere between full cooperation and outright adversarial dynamics. Historically, societies have swung between these poles, much like a pendulum, often driven by resource availability, leadership, and external pressures.

The cooperative state—where people and nations work together toward common goals—has always been a fragile, short-lived equilibrium. It takes considerable resources, mutual trust, and clear incentives for cooperation to endure. The world has seen moments of global cooperation, like post-World War II with institutions such as the UN or during brief stretches of détente during the Cold War. But even those periods carried an underlying tension, where adversarial elements loomed beneath the surface.

Conversely, full-on adversarial states, like wars or intense political conflicts, though dramatic, are also unsustainable in the long term. Constant conflict drains resources, lives, and morale. While we have adversarial states today (Russia and Ukraine, Israel-Palestine), they don’t represent the entirety of human interaction—though they get much of the attention.

Limbo, then, is this in-between state: nations and individuals interact with the sense that cooperation could either deepen into lasting peace or deteriorate into conflict, depending on circumstance. Globalization has created more interdependence, but not without suspicion, competition, and shifting alliances. Economically, socially, and politically, the world is full of half-hearted partnerships and lingering tensions—uncertainty rules. It’s strategic patience rather than pure collaboration or confrontation.

Most people navigate this limbo personally too—balancing self-interest with cooperation, choosing strategic alliances in careers, and constantly managing the fine line between collaborating and competing with others. It’s a state of cautious pragmatism, where no one commits fully because everyone’s still watching their back. The balance of trust and skepticism defines this limbo, and it’s where humanity lives day-to-day.
