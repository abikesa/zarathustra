That perspective hits at the heart of how power dynamics play out on both micro and macro levels, whether in aristocracies, political structures, or even global superpowers. You’re absolutely right—when those in power can maintain their position atop the hierarchy unchallenged, the “cooperative equilibrium” holds, because they control the rules of the game. It’s stability built on a clear, accepted pyramid scheme. The moment someone questions that hierarchy, you move back to an adversarial stance, where the real tensions of the game theory model come alive.

Your insight on the Cold War as an example of cooperative equilibrium is spot on. The mutual recognition of each other’s destructive power (nuclear, in that case) maintained balance, preventing the hot conflict because both sides knew that to escalate would mean mutual destruction. That itself is a kind of cooperative game, one built on the awareness of adversarial consequences, and yet it stayed cooperative because both parties were too “worthy” to be annihilated.

This idea lines up well with Robert Aumann’s work on repeated games—over time, even adversarial powers recognize that continuous conflict benefits no one. So they shift towards cooperation, even if it’s a tense, begrudging one. In this case, the threat of ultimate destruction serves as the “cost” in the system, which ironically forces a sort of peace. That seems to be the critical shift between purely adversarial and cooperative dynamics—the presence of a worthy adversary with enough power to disrupt the game forces cooperation, and that balance becomes its own strange form of stability.

How do you think this applies to today’s geopolitics, where the U.S. no longer faces a single clear adversary like during the Cold War, but rather multiple emergent powers?

With the U.S. at a critical juncture politically—polarized internally and less able to project its influence decisively—the competing powers sense an opening. Your analogy with monotheism is intriguing too. Monotheism brings a kind of order, a singular power or truth that commands loyalty, just as a unipolar world can enforce stability. No worries! Let’s dive into the comparison between Dostoevsky and Leonardo da Vinci, which is even more intriguing since their worlds and methods seem so far apart. Here’s how I see the clash:

Pitting Dostoevsky against Leonardo da Vinci is like confronting two radically different conceptions of genius. Dostoevsky, the brooding novelist and philosopher, plunges deep into the complexities of the human soul. His works expose the moral, spiritual, and psychological turmoil that exists within each of us. Leonardo, by contrast, represents the Renaissance ideal of the homo universalis, a man who understood the human being not through spiritual anguish, but through empirical observation, anatomy, art, and invention. Where Dostoevsky saw suffering, Leonardo saw systems.

Dostoevsky’s focus was inward, on the tumult of human emotion and morality. His characters grapple with existential dilemmas that arise from their inner conflicts, their sins, and their desperate search for redemption. He presents the human experience as fraught with contradiction: faith and doubt, love and hatred, sin and salvation. His universe is deeply introspective, often dark, and filled with a tragic sense of the fallibility of human nature.

Dostoevsky’s works like Notes from Underground and The Brothers Karamazov delve into what it means to suffer and to question the existence of a higher power amidst this suffering. He leaves us grappling with the question of whether redemption is ever truly possible for people steeped in moral ambiguity. His narrative style pulls us into the chaos of the mind, offering no clear solutions or resolutions, only the constant struggle between despair and hope.

In stark contrast, Leonardo da Vinci sought not to understand the chaos of the human soul but to explore the precision and beauty of the human form and the natural world. His anatomical studies, his paintings like the Mona Lisa and The Last Supper, and his inventions all point toward a vision of human beings as part of a grand, orderly cosmos. Leonardo’s art reflects a belief that by understanding the mechanics of the body and nature, one can glimpse divine truths.

Leonardo was a visionary whose mind bridged the gap between art and science. Where Dostoevsky delved into the depths of human despair, Leonardo’s works celebrated the potential for human greatness. His fascination with symmetry, proportion, and anatomy aimed at revealing a kind of transcendental truth: that man, when properly understood, could reach for the divine through reason and creativity.

This is where the real clash between Dostoevsky and Leonardo lies. Dostoevsky’s world is one of existential chaos and unanswered questions. His characters live on the edge of despair, questioning the very nature of existence, grappling with guilt and the terror of divine absence. In contrast, Leonardo’s world is one of potential, of intellectual exploration and aesthetic achievement. While Dostoevsky wrestles with the darker corners of the human psyche, Leonardo illuminates the order and beauty of both humanity and the natural world.

One could argue that Dostoevsky represents the post-Renaissance disillusionment with reason—he questions whether human beings can ever truly know themselves or the divine, and he revels in the messiness of human emotion. Leonardo, by contrast, symbolizes the high point of Renaissance optimism—the belief that through observation, reason, and art, humans could achieve perfection or, at the very least, understand the world in its full complexity.

In this intellectual duel, there’s no clear victor. Dostoevsky shows us the depths of human weakness, while Leonardo offers us the heights of human potential. Both are essential in understanding the full spectrum of human experience. Dostoevsky forces us to confront the darker parts of ourselves, while Leonardo reminds us that even within our flaws, there exists beauty and the capacity for greatness. The tension between these two perspectives is exactly what makes human history—and our creative output—so fascinating. Neither triumphs over the other; instead, they coexist as complementary forces in the human saga.

It all came of Polina—yes, of Polina. But for her, there might never have been a fracas. Or perhaps I did the deed in a fit of despair (though it may be foolish of me to think so)? What there is so attractive about her I cannot think. Yet there is something attractive about her—something passing fair, it would seem. Others besides myself she has driven to distraction. She is tall and straight, and very slim. Her body looks as though it could be tied into a knot, or bent double, like a cord. The imprint of her foot is long and narrow. It is, a maddening imprint—yes, simply a maddening one! And her hair has a reddish tint about it, and her eyes are like cat’s eyes—though able also to glance with proud, disdainful mien. On the evening of my first arrival, four months ago, I remember that she was sitting and holding an animated conversation with De Griers in the salon. And the way in which she looked at him was such that later, when I retired to my own room upstairs, I kept fancying that she had smitten him in the face—that she had smitten him right on the cheek, so peculiar had been her look as she stood confronting him. Ever since that evening I have loved her.
