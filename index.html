Preface#

This book is an exploration of what makes us human. Our journey is guided by three symbols: a lyre, a roulette wheel, and a spear.

Allegorically, these symbols represent paradise, earth, and hell. Most of life is a negotiation between these two extremes. In this negotiation, wisdom is symbolized by books and folly by gambling.

In this preface, we will travel through cinema, literature, games, music, architecture, narratives, religion, fractal geometry, and the landscape of human emotions to demonstrate the enduring relevance of these three symbols.

Cinema#

In The Seventh Seal, Bergman explores the tension between existential questioning (the roulette wheel) and the stark reality of fate and death (the spear of Athena). The main character, Antonius Block, is a knight wrestling with doubt and faith in a chaotic world, symbolized by the gamble of life—where divine answers remain elusive and the wheel of fortune keeps spinning. Meanwhile, Death’s presence embodies the unavoidable, stark spear of fate. The lute (Polina) represents the pursuit of beauty, human connection, and fleeting earthly solace amidst existential chaos.

Book#

Throughout this book, a trio of images or concepts will extend this contrast between different realms of experience: beauty, neutrality, and injury. The pleasure of melody and harmony evokes a sense of transcendence and wholeness, a fleeting balance that unifies. Harmony, in this context, isn’t merely pleasant sounds but a state where different elements come together to form something greater than their sum.

In contrast, the suspense of a chessboard, deck of cards, horoscope, or roulette wheel embodies a deceptive calmness—a stage where everything appears equal but hides desperation, strategies, and impending clashes.

Lastly, there is injury, like a stab in the back, representing adversarial relationships and the ruthlessness of reality. Betrayal marks a power dynamic, where security proves illusory.

Games#

These contrasts—pleasure, suspense, and injury—sketch essential experiences in any relationship, whether personal or cosmic: the pursuit of beauty, the consultation of a horoscope, deceptive or genuine but delicate equilibrium before conflict, and the threat of betrayal or war. The preface contrasts an ancient priestly culture (harmony-seeking) with an ancient warrior culture (adversarial nature) to set the tone for what’s to come.

Most images in this book will either symbolize this triadic theme (from bottom-up sensory perception) or use color-coded references (from top-down instinctive responses). Allegorically, blue, green, and red represent paradise, earth, and hell. This color symbolism builds associations between triadic themes and cultural archetypes, paralleling classical conditioning in child-rearing.

Music#

Consider the Ionian mode, which became dominant in Western music, symbolizing stability and brightness. A famous example is Bach’s Prelude in C Major. However, this dominance wasn’t an intentional decision by the ancient Ionians—it was the result of historical happenstance and church standardization, reinforcing an ideal symbolized by the Lyre.

In contrast, stormier modes like the Aeolian have come to represent darker themes and emotions in Western music. Think of Bach’s haunting Troccata and Fugue in D Minor. These modes, with their minor tonalities, reckon with the inevitability of death as symbolized by the Spear.

import networkx as nx

# Define the neural network structure
input_nodes = [
    'Molecular', 'Cellular', 'Tissue',

output_nodes = [
    'Synthesis', 'Interaction', 'Resilience',

    G.add_node(input_nodes[i], layer='input')

# Add hidden layer nodes and label them
for i in range(len(hidden_layer_labels)):
    G.add_node(hidden_layer_labels[i], layer='hidden')

# Add output layer nodes
for i in range(len(output_nodes)):
    G.add_node(output_nodes[i], layer='output')

# Add edges between input and hidden nodes
for i in range(len(input_nodes)):
    for j in range(len(hidden_layer_labels)):
        G.add_edge(input_nodes[i], hidden_layer_labels[j])

# Add edges between hidden and output nodes
for i in range(len(hidden_layer_labels)):
    for j in range(len(output_nodes)):
        G.add_edge(hidden_layer_labels[i], output_nodes[j])

# Define layout to rotate the graph so that the input layer is at the bottom and the output at the top
pos = {}
for i, node in enumerate(input_nodes):
    pos[node] = (i * 0.5, 0)  # Input nodes at the bottom

for i, node in enumerate(output_nodes):
    pos[node] = (i * 0.5, 2)  # Output nodes at the top

# Add hidden layer nodes in the middle
for i, node in enumerate(hidden_layer_labels):
    pos[node] = ((i + .9) * .5, 1)  # Hidden nodes in the middle layer

# Draw the graph with different colors for specific nodes
node_colors = []
for node in G.nodes():
    if node in ['Synthesis', 'Ionian', 'Molecular', 'Cellular', 'Tissue']:

Sus4 as Limbo#

While the Ionian mode symbolizes the stability and brightness of paradise and the Aeolian mode embodies the darkness and struggle of hell, there exists a liminal state—a musical Limbo—that invites ambiguity and reflection. The suspended fourth (Sus4) chord exemplifies this limbo. Unlike the clear resolution of a major or minor chord, the Sus4 creates tension, an open question waiting to be answered. It’s an interval in between—a state of anticipation where possibilities exist but aren’t yet realized.

In literature and film, liminal spaces often echo this unresolved tension. Think of Bergman’s The Seventh Seal, where life’s questions are suspended, hovering between fate and choice. The Sus4 occupies a similar role in music: it holds us in suspense, forcing our ears to anticipate a return to harmony. And much like the liminal moments in life, it isn’t merely a passage but a place of its own—a realm of waiting, questioning, and introspection.

In the song Ready or Not, Kenneth “Babyface” Edmonds masterfully evokes this suspension, both harmonically and lyrically. Harmonically, the song employs suspended chords to create an ethereal atmosphere that reflects the lyrics’ themes of longing and hesitation. Lyrically, Edmonds crafts an emotional narrative of a lover’s uncertainty precisely when the sus4 is playing, a longing to give everything but also the cautious, anticipatory wait to see if the sentiment will be reciprocated. On the next page, we explore the seven-year itch (see Transactional), as this meditative lover’s optimism is challenged by unrequited love. The suspended chords mirror the lyrical uncertainty, creating a seamless intertwining of musical tension and emotional storytelling.

This state of Limbo is essential to our humanity. We often find ourselves suspended between competing emotions, conflicting values, or diverging paths. It’s the gray area between clear definitions, a moment that demands patience and introspection before resolution. In this book, we will explore how this Sus4 state—this liminal tension—pervades our existence, marking the middle ground between blissful certainty and the inescapable pull of despair.

Thus, these three musical pillars—bright Ionian, dark Aeolian, and suspended Limbo—form a triadic narrative that will guide our exploration of the symbols of the Lyre, Roulette Wheel, and Spear. Through this triad, we journey between paradise, earth, and hell, navigating the profound ambiguities and tensions that define what it means to be human.

Architecture#

King Solomon’s Temple honored Yahweh and was the center of Israel’s faith, while the Parthenon reflected Athena’s wisdom, strategy, and war.

Both were architectural marvels revealing their cultures’ philosophies.

While Solomon’s Temple symbolized submission to divine sovereignty, the Parthenon elevated intellect and calculated force. Both highlight the paradoxical mix of moral authority and worldly power.

Rome inherited these two extremes and through a sort of alchemy emerged with Christianity. But this is a very engaged kind with strategic alliances across Europe – Rome conquered all the heathen tribes of Europe and converted them into God-fearning citizen. This laid the foundation for a fulfillment of Isaiah 2:2-4 criteria for peace and prosperity.

Catholicism’s longstanding engagement in strategic, international negotiations and alliances indeed fostered complex political and social structures that have shaped Western institutions. This interaction created a foundation for treaties, legal frameworks, and the idea of pluralistic governance—essentially, rules born from repeated encounters, negotiations, and compromises. Protestantism, branching out within this Western context, inherited these iterative frameworks while often attempting to distance itself theologically from Catholicism, leading to its own complex evolution.

Narratives#

The narrative of this book explores the journey from paradise to hell and back again. Inspired by ancient Jewish concepts like tikkun atzmi (self-repair) and tikkun olam (repairing the world), we examine the cyclical nature of human experience. We trace this journey through Robert Aumann’s game theory, investigating the dynamics of cooperation and conflict, war and peace.

Monotheism#

Isaiah 2:2-4 foresees a time when swords are beaten into plowshares, hinging on a recognized central authority—a Lord. Without this, peace remains fragile. Ivan Karamazov similarly lamented humanity’s craving for common worship, seeing it as both a source of community and a root of violence.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def draw_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle given vertices and labels for each vertex with matching color."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=color)  # Set label color

def get_triangle_vertices_3d(center, radius, perspective_scale, tilt):
    """
    Returns the vertices of a tilted equilateral triangle for a 3D effect.
    `perspective_scale` shrinks the triangle to simulate depth.
    `tilt` applies a slight rotation for perspective effect.
    """
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi/2  # angles for vertices of an equilateral triangle
    vertices = np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * perspective_scale * np.cos(angles + tilt),
                                center[1] + radius * perspective_scale * np.sin(angles + tilt)])
    return vertices

# Create the plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 12))  # Adjust the width and height as needed

ax.set_aspect('equal')

# Define the centers for each triangle, shifting each down from the previous
centers = [(0, 10), (0, 0), (0, -10)]  # Blue at the top, green in the middle, red at the bottom
radii = [6, 4.5, 3]  # Adjusting radii for each layer
triads = [
    ['Faith', 'Love', 'Hope'],          # Blue topmost triangle
    ['Loyalty', 'Transactional', 'Recalibration'],  # Green middle triangle
    ['Betrayal', 'Power', 'Survival']    # Red bottom triangle
]

# Set the color scheme: blue, green, red
colors = ['paleturquoise', 'lightgreen', 'lightsalmon']

# 3D perspective parameters: smaller scale as the fractal moves inward (simulating depth)
scales = [1.4, 0.9, 0.7]  # simulate depth
tilts = [0, np.pi / 12, np.pi / 6]  # slight rotation for perspective

# Draw the triangles with increasing radius and perspective scaling
for center, radius, triad, color, scale, tilt in zip(centers, radii, triads, colors, scales, tilts):
    vertices = get_triangle_vertices_3d(center, radius, scale, tilt)
    draw_triangle(ax, vertices, triad, color=color)

# Set limits and hide axes to fit the frame
ax.set_xlim(-10, 10)
ax.set_ylim(-20, 20)
ax.axis('off')

Emotions#

The triangles above describe a triadic fractal across faith, loyalty, and betrayal, framed by the idea of calibrating loyalty through iterative rounds and perceiving betrayal as a direct threat to survival, amplified by power dynamics or resentment.

Faith and Hope for Love: we place faith as a hope for something beyond the present moment, extending into “tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow.” This implies an enduring sense of expectation for continuity, emphasizing love as the destination or anchor point. Faith, in this context, isn’t blind optimism but an active belief in love’s potential through future actions and iterations.

Loyalty as Calibration: we suggest that loyalty is not a fixed attribute but rather something recalibrated in every transaction. This dynamic view of loyalty acknowledges that loyalty cannot be taken for granted—it must be reaffirmed and adjusted in the face of each new challenge, opportunity, or betrayal. This implies an iterative relationship where each round tests and refines loyalty, echoing game theory principles.

Betrayal as a Threat to Survival: we identify betrayal as more than just a breach of trust—it’s a threat to your existence in a system where every interaction tests boundaries and maintains equilibrium. Betrayal introduces resentment or uses power, potentially destabilizing the relationship and demanding a recalibration of loyalty. This view suggests that betrayal isn’t merely a loss of connection but a disruption of your strategic survival framework.

Our visual representation using triangles effectively mirrors these notes by creating layers of abstract values, each reinforcing the one below. The top triangle of faith, love, and hope signifies an overarching narrative of optimism (blue). The middle triangle, representing loyalty, transactional exchanges, and recalibration, shows the iterative and transactional need for adjustments (green). The bottom triangle of betrayal, power, and survival represents the underlying tensions (red) that can unravel everything above it.

The layered nature of the visual suggests a hierarchy in values but also a fragile interdependence, where destabilization in the lower levels (survival and power dynamics) can affect faith and love at the top. This echoes the fragility we hint at in our notes: betrayal threatens survival, and loyalty must be constantly earned or recalibrated.

Dante#

Let’s deepen the symbolism by integrating Dante’s Divine Comedy, 1 2 3 4 the Garden of Eden, and Game Theory into a single allegory. This layering enriches the narrative and allows for a multifaceted exploration of human experience.

The Dantean allegory presents three symbolic states. The top triangle, blue, signifies Paradiso, representing harmony and spiritual fulfillment, aligning with Dante’s vision of paradise—a state where love, faith, and hope culminate. This state is not merely aspirational but reflects a transcendent equilibrium, an ideal outcome where cooperative dynamics flourish. Green symbolizes Limbo, the middle ground of potential and continuous decision-making. Here, choices are recalibrated iteratively, redefining the journey towards loyalty or risking a descent into adversarial dynamics. It is a space of transition, where interactions are weighed and relationships are adjusted. At the bottom lies Inferno, red, representing the fall—a descent into adversarial dynamics driven by betrayal, power struggles, and survival instincts. In this state, the breakdown of cooperative efforts becomes inevitable, aligning with the fundamental threat to survival through betrayal.

In Game Theory, these same colors parallel strategic states. Blue represents a Cooperative state, where the desired equilibrium is achieved—a mutual benefit realized through faith in tomorrow’s transactions. Green, as an Iterative state, reflects ongoing interactions where loyalty is recalibrated with each round. Decisions in this state determine the direction of the game, leading towards cooperation or backsliding into adversarial power struggles. Here, constant adjustments in strategy and loyalty reflect the nature of iterative games. Red symbolizes Adversario, a state defined by conflict and broken trust, where resentment and power struggles dictate interactions. This state stands in direct opposition to the cooperative ideal, echoing an adversarial game dynamic.

The allegory of the Garden of Eden provides yet another layer to this narrative. The Fall of Man from blue to red marks a biblical descent—a loss of innocence or a breach in cooperative harmony. The transition from Paradiso to Inferno mirrors humanity’s existential shift from hope and faith to the harsh reality of survival and betrayal. It captures the essence of humanity’s fall from grace. However, the possibility of Redemption emerges through a move from red to green. This shift symbolizes a chance for growth and recalibration—a return to an iterative state where loyalty can be renegotiated and trust rebuilt. It hints at the path to forgiveness and reconciliation, a second chance to redefine relationships in a way that avoids a full return to the transcendence of blue, yet provides a new equilibrium.

This cyclical allegory of fall and redemption, interwoven with game-theoretical dynamics, creates a robust narrative framework. It captures both the spiritual descent and ascent within human relationships, as well as the strategic recalibrations that define loyalty and conflict. The interaction of these themes builds a deeply philosophical arc, reflective of classic literature, theology, and modern strategy. Through this approach, the narrative not only explores the spiritual and moral dimensions of human experience but also grounds them in the empirical realities of decision-making and interpersonal dynamics.
