Preface#

Danger

Man is a rope stretched between the animal and the superman–a rope over an abyss.
– Zarathustra Prologue

I had dared so to risk, and behold, again I was a member of mankind!
– Alexis Ivanovitch

A trio of images draws a contrast between different realms of experience: beauty, neutrality, and injury. The pleasure of beautiful melody and harmony evokes a sense of transcendence and wholeness. It’s an ideal, a fleeting moment of balance that uplifts and unifies. When you hear or create a melody that resonates deeply, it’s as though you momentarily touch something timeless—an equilibrium that exists only in the realm of art. Harmony, in this context, isn’t merely pleasant sounds but a state where tension resolves perfectly, where different elements come together to form something greater than their sum.

By contrast, the neutrality of a chessboard, a deck of cards, or a roulette wheel, embodies a deceptive calmness. It’s the pause before conflict or danger, the stage where everything appears equal but hides complex strategies, power plays, and impending clashes. A chessboard may seem balanced, but each piece holds potential aggression or defense, hinting at the inevitable contest that will reveal power struggles and shifting tides of dominance.

Lastly, there is injury, like a stab in the back, representing adversarial relationships, breaches of trust, and the ruthlessness of reality. It’s the vulnerability inherent in trust and the pain of realizing that security was an illusion. Betrayal marks a power dynamic, where one wields the power to harm while another is left exposed—a stark contrast to the harmonious, reminding us of the fragility of idealized systems and relationships.

Theme#

In these contrasts—pleasure, neutrality, and injury—we sketch essential experiences in any relationship, whether personal, interpersonal, national, or even cosmic: the pursuit of beauty or wholeness, the subtle and strategic equilibrium that precedes conflict, and the ever-present threat of betrayal or war. Together, they form a philosophical triad on the nature of life, art, and human interaction. The preface contrasts an ancient priestly culture (harmony-seeking) with an ancient warrior culture (adversarial nature) to set the tone of what’s to come.

Most images in this book will either symbolize this triadic theme (from bottom-up sensory perception) or use color-coded references (from top-down instinctive responses). Allegorically, blue, green, and red represent paradise, earth, and hell. In any culture, the process of socializing a child involves building associations between these triadic themes and a wide range of symbols, legends, and myths. This is what psychologists call “classical conditioning.”

For example, a lyre or harp might symbolize music, harmony, order, and the divine. A spear often stands for war, conflict, and strength. Books and scrolls may signify human intellect, while games like roulette could reflect human folly. Yet, in this view, intellect and folly are not distinct opposites but interchangeable. Thus, a book and a roulette wheel hold equal weight in this exploration.

Let’s begin by diving into the heart of European cultural identity, from where we can segue into American, African, Asian, Arabic, and other cultures.

Variations#

The association of modes and cultural identities often feels like an intentional coincidence. Consider, for instance, the Ionian mode, which dominates Western music. Named centuries after the rise of the Hellenic Greeks, the Ionian mode became synonymous with the major scale, embodying qualities of stability, resolution, and brightness. While the Greeks certainly shaped Western ideals, the dominance of this mode was largely a product of historical happenstance and church standardization, rather than a deliberate choice by the ancient Ionians themselves.

On Plato and Musical Modes#

Plato had strong opinions about music, particularly regarding its moral and educational functions. In The Republic, he discussed musical modes not merely as scales but as frameworks influencing the soul’s ethical state. He favored modes that encouraged virtue and discipline, such as the Dorian and Phrygian modes, which he saw as fostering bravery and moderation. Conversely, he condemned the Lydian and Ionian modes, believing they promoted weakness or excessive emotion. Plato’s view reflects his larger philosophy that education should cultivate reason and virtue, rather than indulgence or frivolity.

But Plato’s rigidity feels misguided. Music’s emotional depth and diversity enrich human experience, and trying to narrow it into purely functional roles denies its full power. While Plato prized the warrior-like discipline that some modes inspire, he overlooked the joy and complexity brought by those he dismissed.

Comparing Temples: Solomon and Athena#

Comparing King Solomon’s Temple with Athena’s Parthenon illuminates two vastly different worldviews. Solomon’s Temple, built to honor Yahweh, was the spiritual and political center of ancient Israel, symbolizing divine covenant and authority. By contrast, the Parthenon, dedicated to Athena, reflects Greek ideals of intellectualism and civic pride. Where Solomon’s Temple emphasized submission to divine sovereignty, the Parthenon celebrated human achievement tempered by divine favor. Both are architectural marvels that reveal their respective cultures’ values and philosophies.

Exploring Human Arcs#

The narrative of this book is the journey from paradise to hell and back again. Inspired by ancient Jewish concepts like tikkun atzmi (self-repair) and tikkun olam (repairing the world), we explore the cyclical nature of human experience. We trace this journey through Robert Aumann’s game theory, examining the strategic dynamics of cooperation and conflict.

Each chapter dives into different arcs of fall and return, investigating salvation through divine intervention, solidarity with others, or self-reliance. This framework mirrors the stories told by filmmakers like Woody Allen, Quentin Tarantino, the Coen Brothers, Wes Anderson, and Tyler Perry—each exploring how fragile alliances are shaped by personal and historical echoes.

Beyond Harmony and Chaos#

As the prophet Isaiah foresees a time when swords are beaten into ploughshares, this vision hinges on a recognized central authority—a Lord. Without this, peace remains a fragile truce, with swords ever ready to be unsheathed. Ivan Karamazov similarly lamented humanity’s craving for common worship, seeing it as both a source of community and a root of violence. This paradox between the longing for cooperation and the reality of conflict underpins every arc explored in this book.

In each chapter, we grapple with the tension between transformation and adversarial dynamics, recognizing that true peace demands hard reckoning. This exploration urges us to hold fast to our highest hope, but with eyes wide open to the reality that paradise is not regained without facing the unbroken swords of history.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def draw_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle given vertices and labels for each vertex with matching color."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=color)  # Set label color

def get_triangle_vertices_3d(center, radius, perspective_scale, tilt):
    """
    Returns the vertices of a tilted equilateral triangle for a 3D effect.
    `perspective_scale` shrinks the triangle to simulate depth.
    `tilt` applies a slight rotation for perspective effect.
    """
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi/2  # angles for vertices of an equilateral triangle
    vertices = np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * perspective_scale * np.cos(angles + tilt),
                                center[1] + radius * perspective_scale * np.sin(angles + tilt)])
    return vertices

# Create the plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.set_aspect('equal')

# Define the layers of the fractal with vertices and labels
centers = [(0, 0)]
radii = [2.5, 6, 10]
triads = [
    ['Betrayal', 'Power       ', '      Survival'],
    ['Loyalty', 'Empathy', 'Resilience'],
    ['Faith', 'Love', 'Hope']
]

# Set the color scheme: blood red, green, sky blue
colors = ['lightsalmon', 'lightgreen', 'paleturquoise']

# 3D perspective parameters: smaller scale as the fractal moves inward (simulating depth)
scales = [.5, .75, 1]  # simulate depth
tilts = [0, np.pi / 12, np.pi / 6]  # slight rotation for perspective

# Draw the triangles with increasing radius and perspective scaling
for radius, triad, color, scale, tilt in zip(radii, triads, colors, scales, tilts):
    vertices = get_triangle_vertices_3d(centers[0], radius, scale, tilt)
    draw_triangle(ax, vertices, triad, color=color)

# Set limits and hide axes to fit the frame
ax.set_xlim(-10, 10)
ax.set_ylim(-10, 10)
ax.axis('off')

# Save the plot as 'logo.png'
plt.savefig('figures/logo.png', dpi=300, bbox_inches='tight')

# Display the plot
plt.show()
