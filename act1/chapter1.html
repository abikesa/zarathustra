Eternal Recurrence#

One author is born in 1818 somewhere in Russia. And then reborn in 1844 somewhere in Germany. They both loath Germans. Let‚Äôs get right to it. If we‚Äôre talking about which of Dostoevsky‚Äôs works Nietzsche might have read or resonated with most deeply, it‚Äôs pretty clear from his own writing that he held Notes from Underground and Crime and Punishment in high regard. Nietzsche explicitly praised Dostoevsky as ‚Äúthe only psychologist from whom I had something to learn,‚Äù which is a high compliment from someone as fiercely self-assured as Nietzsche.

Notes from Underground is likely to have struck a deep chord with Nietzsche due to its rejection of rationalism and the idea of human beings as purely rational creatures. The Underground Man‚Äôs insistence on suffering as an assertion of freedom aligns closely with Nietzsche‚Äôs critique of the comfortable, unexamined lives that most people lead‚Äîwhat he might call ‚Äúslave morality.‚Äù The way the Underground Man almost celebrates his self-inflicted suffering would resonate with Nietzsche‚Äôs appreciation for embracing struggle and hardship as a path to self-discovery ‚Äì which to both men is self-love. import networkx as nx
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Create a directed graph (DAG)
G = nx.DiGraph()

# Add nodes and edges based on the neuron structure
G.add_edges_from([(1, 4), (2, 4), (3, 4), (4, 5), (5, 6)])

# Define positions for each node
pos = {1: (0, 2), 2: (1, 2), 3: (2, 2), 4: (1, 1), 5: (1, 0), 6: (1, -1)}

# Labels to reflect parts of a neuron
labels = {
    1: 'Diet', 
    2: 'Locality', 
    3: 'Recreation', 
    4: 'Self-Love',
    5: 'Play', 
    6: 'Vitality'
}

# Define node colors
node_colors = ['lemonchiffon', 'paleturquoise', 'mistyrose', 'thistle', 'lightgreen', 'lightsalmon']

# Create a figure and axis with a specified size
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 8))  # Adjust width and height as needed

# Draw the nodes and edges first with turquoise edges
nx.draw(G, pos, with_labels=False, node_size=2000, node_color=node_colors, ax=ax, arrows=True, edge_color='turquoise')

# Draw the labels with the color of the previous node
for i, (node, (x, y)) in enumerate(pos.items()):
    previous_color = node_colors[i - 1] if i > 0 else node_colors[-1]  # Shift colors
    ax.text(x, y, labels[node], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=previous_color)

# Set the title
plt.title("Tragic Hero")

# Display the plot
plt.show()

However, Crime and Punishment might have also grabbed Nietzsche‚Äôs attention, particularly in Raskolnikov‚Äôs moral dilemmas and the exploration of a ‚Äúsuperman‚Äù who places himself above common morality. Raskolnikov‚Äôs internal battle‚Äîhis belief that he can transcend ordinary morality, and his subsequent downfall‚Äîmirrors Nietzsche‚Äôs thoughts on the √úbermensch, but also serves as a cautionary tale of its darker potential. Nietzsche would have appreciated the existential crisis Raskolnikov experiences, even if he might critique his failure to fully embrace his own power and freedom.

If Nietzsche read only one of Dostoevsky‚Äôs works, I‚Äôd put my money on Notes from Underground being the most impactful, but Crime and Punishment is a close contender for its thematic overlap with Nietzsche‚Äôs explorations of morality, power, and the individual‚Äôs struggle against societal norms.
