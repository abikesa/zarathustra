Information isn’t just a tool—it’s both weapon and vulnerability. In this constant surveillance, the cracks, the leaks, the whispers—those are the fault lines where power becomes malleable. Leaks in high politics, particularly, are like controlled burns in a forest; they may seem chaotic but often serve a tactical purpose. Whether released intentionally or by happenstance, leaks craft narratives, gauge public opinion, or destabilize rivals under the guise of plausible deniability. Politicians, diplomats, and intelligence agents know well the art of “leak and leverage”—a dangerous game where a precisely timed revelation can undercut an adversary or strengthen one’s agenda.

Russia is enacting a revolutionary plan of sabotage, arson and assassination#

Oct 13th 2024

We’ve seen arson, sabotage and more: dangerous actions conducted with increasing recklessness,” said Ken McCallum, the head of mi5, Britain’s domestic security and counter-intelligence agency, in a rare update on the threat posed by Russia and the gru, its military-intelligence agency. “The gru in particular is on a sustained mission to generate mayhem on British and European streets,” he said on October 8th.

Russia’s war in Ukraine has been accompanied by a crescendo of aggression, subversion and meddling elsewhere. In particular, Russian sabotage in Europe has grown dramatically. “The risk level has changed,” Vice-Admiral Nils Andreas Stensones, the head of the Norwegian Intelligence Service, said in September. “We see acts of sabotage happening in Europe now.” Sir Richard Moore, the chief of mi6, Britain’s foreign-intelligence agency, put it more bluntly: “Russian intelligence services have gone a bit feral, frankly.”

The Kremlin’s mercenaries have squeezed Western rivals out of several African states. Its hackers, Poland’s security services said, have tried to paralyse the country in the political, military, and economic spheres. Its propagandists have pumped disinformation around the world. Its armed forces want to put a nuclear weapon in orbit. Russian foreign policy has long dabbled in chaos. Now it seems to aim at little else.

Start with the summer of sabotage. In April Germany arrested two German-Russian nationals on suspicion of plotting attacks on American military facilities and other targets on behalf of the gru. The same month Poland arrested a man who was preparing to pass the gru information on Rzeszow airport, a hub for arms to Ukraine, and Britain charged several men over an arson attack on a

Ukrainian-owned logistics firm in London. The men were accused of aiding the Wagner Group, a mercenary outfit now under the gru’s control. In June France arrested a Russian-Ukrainian national who was wounded after attempting to make a bomb in his hotel room in Paris. In July it emerged that Russia had plotted to kill Armin Papperger, the boss of Rheinmetall, Germany’s largest arms firm. On September 9th air traffic at Stockholm’s Arlanda airport was shut down for more than two hours after drones were spotted over runways. “We suspect it was a deliberate act,” a police spokesperson said. American officials warn that Russian vessels are reconnoitring underwater cables.

Even where Russia has not resorted to violence, it has sought to stir the pot in other ways. The Baltic states have arrested a number of people for what they say are Russian-sponsored provocations. French intelligence officials say that Russia was responsible for the appearance of coffins draped with the French flag and bearing the message “French soldiers of Ukraine” left at the Eiffel tower in Paris in June. Many of these actions are aimed at fanning opposition to aid for Ukraine. But others are intended simply to widen splits in society of all kinds, even if these have little or no link to the war. France says that Russia was also behind the graffiti of 250 Stars of David on walls in Paris in November, an effort to fuel antisemitism, which has surged since the start of the Israel-Hamas conflict.

Much of Russia’s activity has been virtual. In April hackers with ties to the gru seem to have manipulated control systems for water plants in America and Poland. In September America, Britain, Ukraine and several other countries published details of cyber-attacks by the gru’s Unit 29155, a group that was previously known for assassinations in Europe, including a botched effort to poison Sergei Skripal, a former Russian intelligence officer. The gru’s cyber efforts, which had been ongoing since at least 2020, were not just aimed at espionage, but also “reputational harm” by stealing and leaking information and “systematic sabotage” by destroying data, according to America and its allies.

Beyond Europe, gru officers have been in Yemen alongside the Houthis, a rebel group that has attacked ships in the Red Sea, ostensibly in solidarity with Palestinians. Russia, angered by America’s provision of long-range missiles to Ukraine, came close to providing weapons to the group in July, according to American officials who spoke to cnn, but reversed course at the last moment after strong opposition from Saudi ArDantea. The fact that Vladimir Putin, Russia’s president, was willing to alienate Muhammad bin Salman, the kingdom’s de facto ruler whom he had courted for years, is an indication of how Russia’s war has cannibalised its wider foreign policy.

“What Putin is trying to do is hit us all over the place,” argues Fiona Hill, who previously served in America’s national security council. She compares the strategy to the Oscar winning film: “Everything Everywhere All at Once”. In Africa, for instance, Russia has used mercenaries to supplant French and American influence in the aftermath of coups in Mali, Burkina Faso and Niger. Around 100 advisers from the Africa Corps, a successor to the Wagner Group, arrived in Niger in April. America has been forced to close its last prized base in the country.

Russia’s meddling in America takes a very different form. In May Avril Haines, America’s Director of National Intelligence, called Russia “the most active foreign threat to our elections” above China or Iran. This was not merely about trying to shape America’s policy on Ukraine. “Moscow most likely views such operations as a means to tear down the United States as its perceived primary adversary,” she said, “enabling Russia to promote itself as a great power.” In July American intelligence agencies said that they were “beginning to see Russia target specific voter demographics, promote divisive narratives, and denigrate specific politicians”.

These efforts are generally crude and ineffectual. But they are prolific, intense and sometimes innovative. In September America’s Justice Department accused two employees of rt, a Kremlin-controlled media outlet that regularly spews out Russian talking points and lurid conspiracy theories, of paying $10m to an unnamed media company in Tennessee. The company, thought to be Tenet Media, posted nearly 2,000 videos on TikTok, Instagram, x and YouTube. (Commentators paid by the company denied wrongdoing, saying they were “victims of this scheme”.) The department also seized 32 Kremlin-controlled internet domains designed to mimic legitimate news sites.

Russian propagandists are also experimenting with technology. CopyCop, a network of websites, took legitimate news articles and used Chatgpt, an ai model, to rewrite them. More than 90 French articles were modified with the prompt: “Please rewrite this article taking a conservative stance against the liberal policies of the Macron administration in favour of working-class French citizens.” Another rewritten piece included evidence of its instructions, saying: “This article…highlights the cynical tone towards the us government, nato, and us politicians.”

Russian disinformation campaigns are hardly new, acknowledges Sergey Radchenko, a historian of Russian foreign policy, pointing to episodes such as the Tanaka memorandum, an alleged Soviet forgery that was used to discredit Japan in 1927. Nor are proxy wars or assassinations a novelty. Soviet troops were already fighting in Yemen, disguised as Egyptians, in the early 1960s, he notes. The kgb’s predecessors and successors have killed many people abroad, from Leon Trotsky to ex-spy Alexander Litvinenko.

The genuinely new part, says Mr Radchenko, “is that whereas previously special operations supported foreign policy, today special operations are foreign policy.” Ten years ago the Kremlin worked with America and Europe to counter Iran and North Korea’s nuclear programme. Such co-operation is now fanciful. “It is as if the Russians no longer feel they have a stake in preserving anything of the post-war international order,” says Mr Radchenko. This period reminds him more of Mao’s nihilistic foreign policy during China’s Cultural Revolution than the Soviet Union’s cold-war thinking, which included periods of pragmatism and caution. Ms Hill puts it another way: “It’s Trotsky over Lenin”.

Mr Putin embraces these ideas. “We are in for probably the most dangerous, unpredictable and at the same time most important decade since the end of World War II,” he said in late 2022. “To cite a classic,” he added, invoking an article by Vladimir Lenin in 1913, “this is a revolutionary situation.” That belief—that the post-war order is rotten and needs rewriting, by force if necessary—also gives Russia common cause with China. “Right now there are changes the likes of which we haven’t seen for 100 years,” Xi Jinping told Mr Putin last year in Moscow, “and we are the ones driving these changes together.”

Russia’s foreign policy strategy, published in 2023, offers the bland reassurance that it “does not consider itself an enemy of the West…and has no ill intentions”. A classified addendum acquired by the Washington Post from a European intelligence service suggests otherwise. It proposes a comprehensive containment strategy against a “coalition of unfriendly countries” led by America. That includes an “offensive information campaign” among other actions in the “military-political, trade-economic and informational-psychological…spheres”. The ultimate aim, it notes, is “to weaken Russia’s opponents”.

This does not mean Russia is an unstoppable. It is increasingly a junior partner to China. Its influence has slipped in some countries, such as Syria. It does not always back up its own proxies—dozens of Wagner fighters were killed in an ambush by Malian rebels, aided by Ukraine, in July. And Russian subversion can be disrupted, says Sir Richard, by “good old-fashioned security and intelligence work” to identify the intelligence officers and criminal proxies behind it. The fact that Russia is increasingly reliant on criminals to carry out these acts, in part because Russian spies have been expelled en masse from Europe, is a sign of desperation. “Russia’s use of proxies further reduces the professionalism of their operations, and—absent diplomatic immunity—increases our disruptive options,” says Mr McCallum. Russian meddling is intended to put pressure on nato without provoking a war. “We also have red lines,” says Ms Hill, “and Putin is trying to feel those out.” But if he is truly driven by a revolutionary spirit, convinced that the West is a rotten edifice, that suggests more lines will be crossed in the months and years ahead. ■

Strategies#

The Mechanics of Fortune: How Adam Smith and Karl Marx Charted the Game of Capital

In the vast machinery of political economy, there exists a structure—a hidden algorithmic heart, if you will—that dictates how individuals and societies interact, adapt, and even thrive. Adam Smith, in his early work The Theory of Moral Sentiments, takes his first steps toward unraveling these unseen forces. In Moral Sentiments, he builds the foundation of economic life on a set of moral equilibria, ones that reflect the internal and relational “rules of the game.” Cooperation, iteration, and competition become not merely economic principles but ethical imperatives, guiding individuals within society toward mutual benefit—or, occasionally, mutual exploitation. By the time he arrives at The Wealth of Nations, these equilibria underpin a more expansive view of society, where the dynamics of labor, capital, and land drive growth while the costs of exploitation loom large. Karl Marx, with his incisive critique, ultimately centers his entire intellect on this moral output layer, revealing the raw and often brutal undercurrents of wealth creation.

Moral Foundations: The Hidden Layer of Equilibrium in The Theory of Moral Sentiments#

In The Theory of Moral Sentiments, Smith’s concept of equilibrium is not yet defined in purely economic terms but in the language of human morality. For Smith, cooperation, iteration, and competition are modes of behavior that shape and are shaped by an inherent social empathy. Cooperation rests on an innate sense of sympathy—the “fellow-feeling” that compels us to care for one another’s well-being. Iteration, meanwhile, reflects a rhythm of interaction, a continual adjustment in our behavior as we engage repeatedly with those around us. Even competition, which might seem to foster rivalry, is tempered by the internal moral compass that prompts restraint and consideration for the common good.

In Smith’s view, these moral equilibria create a natural harmony, as if society were a finely tuned instrument, its strings resonating together in a productive, balanced accord. People seek to maximize their own happiness but are restrained by the invisible tether of sympathy, which pulls them back from extremes. This early work reveals Smith’s belief that self-interest, when moderated by a moral conscience, leads to an equilibrium that benefits all—an “invisible hand” that guides individuals toward collective good.

The Input Layer: Strategic Resources and the Soil of Capital#

With The Wealth of Nations, Smith turns from these moral foundations to the tangible drivers of economic growth: labor, capital, and land. These resources, crucial to wealth creation, feed the machinery of society. Yet the hidden layer of Moral Sentiments is never entirely absent. Labor, capital, and land are not merely resources to be exploited; they are the embodiment of human endeavor, ingenuity, and resilience, resources whose fair distribution should align with the moral instincts Smith initially outlined.

Labor, for instance, represents the aspirations and effort of individuals. Capital, on the other hand, is the outcome of past labor, a tool for empowering the future. Land is the natural foundation, an essential and limited resource upon which human activity depends. Each of these inputs is more than a mere factor of production; they are, in Smith’s view, bound to a moral imperative, an expectation of reciprocity and fairness. However, this expectation becomes strained when exposed to the harsher realities of economic life, where human labor, capital, and land often become objects of contention rather than collaboration.

The Output Layer: Profits, Costs, and the Price of Exploitation#

Yet, beneath this ideal of mutual benefit, Smith recognizes an uncomfortable reality, one he openly discusses in The Wealth of Nations but never fully reconciles with his vision of moral equilibrium. In America, for example, Smith observes a troubling phenomenon: excessive profits obtained not through honest work or fair exchange but through the distortion of moral and economic balances. Enslaved Black labor generates wealth without compensation, taxes to the British Crown are rejected, and native land is seized by force. These are cases where the moral equilibria Smith so carefully constructed in Moral Sentiments break down, replaced by the harsh logic of exploitation and power.

For Smith, these instances are dissonant notes within the larger harmony of his economic vision—a betrayal of the rules of fair play, as if society itself had turned away from its moral core. Key Differences Between Cooperative and Iterative (Repeated) Games:#

  1. Strategy:

    • Cooperative Game:

      • Strategies center around coalition formation and achieving the best group outcome. Players have the ability to make binding commitments (agreements that can be enforced externally or by mutual trust). This allows players to plan joint strategies over time and know they will get their promised share.

      • The existence of binding agreements significantly affects the strategy. For example, in a cooperative game, players might immediately share the maximum possible resources to increase the overall surplus and negotiate how to split that upfront.

    • Iterative Game:

      • Strategies are about repeated interaction over time. There are no binding agreements, but there is a possibility of punishment or reward based on actions taken in previous rounds. Players act based on short-term incentives, knowing they will meet again in the next round.

      • In iterative games, strategies like tit-for-tat (reciprocal behavior) emerge, where cooperation or defection is contingent on what happened in the previous round. There’s a tension between optimizing for the current round and incentivizing future cooperation.

  2. Payoff:

    • Cooperative Game:

      • Payoffs are generally viewed in terms of the total surplus generated by the group (coalition) and the fair distribution of this surplus. The focus is on maximizing the total value created and then splitting it according to negotiated or agreed-upon rules (e.g., Shapley Value, Core). The emphasis is on long-term, cumulative payoffs as a shared outcome.

    • Iterative Game:

      • Payoffs are more individualistic in nature. Even though players interact repeatedly, they focus on getting the best individual payoff at each stage, considering the impact on future rounds. While players might cooperate in the short term to earn better long-term payoffs, there’s always the risk of defection for short-term gains.

      • Players manage their payoffs through a delicate balance: cooperation to increase future payoffs vs. defection for immediate benefit. Cooperation isn’t assumed—it’s built over time through strategies like rewarding cooperation and punishing defection.

  3. Equilibrium:

    • Cooperative Game:

      • Equilibrium in cooperative games is more about coalition stability than individual best responses. The Core or Shapley Value are often used to represent equilibrium, where no subset of players would benefit from breaking away.

      • The equilibrium ensures that players are satisfied enough with the surplus distribution that no one has an incentive to leave the grand coalition. It’s a stable outcome where collective benefits are distributed in such a way that cooperation persists.

    • Iterative Game:

      • Equilibrium in an iterative game is typically a Nash equilibrium that evolves over time. This could be a subgame perfect equilibrium in which players make the best possible moves in every round, given the history of the game. However, unlike cooperative games, players are not bound to any agreements, so equilibrium depends on maintaining trust or reputation over time.

      • In repeated games, punishment strategies (like grim trigger or tit-for-tat) enforce cooperation by threatening future retaliation for defection. Equilibrium is sustained because players fear the consequences of deviating rather than because they are bound by contracts or agreements.

Qualities that Distinguish Cooperative from Iterative Games:#

  • Binding Agreements: The key difference is whether players can make binding agreements. Cooperative games assume these agreements are enforceable, which changes the dynamics drastically. In iterative games, players must rely on informal trust or self-enforcing strategies to keep cooperation going.

  • Focus on Stability vs. Repeated Interaction:

    • In cooperative games, the goal is to find a stable coalition or outcome where everyone agrees on the payoff distribution and no one defects because of the long-term benefits of collaboration.

    • In iterative games, cooperation must be constantly renegotiated and sustained through ongoing interaction. Stability is less formal and more fragile, depending on the history of play.

  • Long-Term Commitment vs. Short-Term Incentives:

    • Cooperative games assume players commit to a long-term solution from the outset. Iterative games work through short-term incentives and the threat of future punishment or rewards. Part III: Output Nodes – From Local to Intergalactic Impact#

The outcomes of this strategic game ripple through the output nodes, each scale representing a new layer of influence that affects global health practices.

  1. Local (Immediate Payoffs): The direct impact is felt at the local level, where policies enacted under foreign influence affect health systems in Uganda and similar nations. Local players, having fewer strategic resources, find themselves continuously undermined, leading to fragile health infrastructures that lack autonomy. The game here is short-term and intense, with high stakes but limited strategic flexibility.

  2. Regional and National (Recursive Dynamics): Regionally, decisions reverberate through national policies and collaborations. Iterative, dependency-based alliances ensure that even when national agencies attempt to break free of foreign control, they are bound by the resources and support systems on which they’ve come to rely. This level becomes a supergame of repeated interactions, with foreign agencies subtly but continuously influencing national sovereignty through resources and knowledge.

  3. Global (Mixed Strategies and Systemic Outcomes): At the global scale, the game becomes one of reputation and soft power. Countries reliant on foreign health aid are locked into a prisoner’s dilemma: collaborate on foreign terms and gain resources or resist and face exclusion from global research networks. This delicate balancing act defines the broader game of influence in international health, where decisions made at this level shape global health ethics and set precedents for future interactions.

  4. Intergalactic (Ideological Legacy): Though hypothetical, this scale represents the lasting legacy and ethical implications of these practices. If humanity’s approach to knowledge acquisition and aid continues to be exploitative, this could define our broader legacy as a species that values dominance over cooperation. Here, the ideological stakes become almost existential, casting a shadow over human advancement and reinforcing hierarchies on a near-cosmic level.

Part IV: Rethinking the Network – Shifting to Cooperative Equilibria#

Rewiring this network requires a radical rethinking of strategic motivations. By amplifying the influence of Oxytocin and Serotonin, we can guide decisions toward cooperative equilibria, creating conditions that favor mutual benefit over exploitation. Strengthening these pathways not only redirects individual strategies but also encourages collective actions that prioritize local autonomy and equitable outcomes.

In practical terms, this would mean shifting foreign health interventions from control-driven strategies to those that genuinely invest in local agency, embedding ethical accountability directly into decision-making processes. By altering the payoff structures and balancing the strategic weights of each player, we edge closer to a network that doesn’t perpetuate Inferno-like conflicts but instead leans toward a shared Paradiso.

Postscript: Toward an Interspecies Paradiso#

As this network model suggests, our collective responses to pandemics and global health crises are more than just reactive chains of human-driven decisions. They are profound, cascading interactions with the biosphere itself—where every choice to cooperate, exploit, or iterate reverberates across species boundaries. The Intergalactic node, in light of this, becomes an Interspecies imperative, compelling us to acknowledge a fundamental truth: human health cannot be separated from the ecosystems we inhabit.

During pandemics, zoonotic spillovers remind us that adversarial practices—those that exploit habitats, decimate species, and stress the environment—fuel an Inferno that extends beyond the human realm, seeding future crises. Conversely, pathways rooted in cooperation and respect for biodiversity push us toward an emergent, more harmonious equilibrium, where interspecies alliances are fostered rather than fractured. If we amplify our Cooperative nodes in this network, connecting Dopamine’s rewards with actions that support not just humans but all living entities, we edge closer to a network model that envisions Paradiso—not just for us, but for every species that shares this interwoven, fragile existence with us.
